Percentage Calculator

What is a Percentage Calculator?

A percentage calculator helps you determine the value that corresponds to a specific percentage of a given total. It's useful for discounts, grades, tax calculations, and financial evaluations.

What is a Percentage?

A percentage is a number or ratio expressed as a fraction of 100. The symbol "%" means "per hundred." It helps express proportions, changes, comparisons, and trends in an intuitive way. For example, saying “50% of a class passed” means 50 out of every 100 students passed. Percentages are widely used in finance, sales, data analysis, health, academics, and daily life.

Basic Percentage Formulas

  • Percentage of a Number: (Percentage ÷ 100) × Number
  • What percent of X is Y? (Y ÷ X) × 100
  • Percentage Increase: ((New - Original) ÷ Original) × 100
  • Percentage Decrease: ((Original - New) ÷ Original) × 100
  • Find the original number from a percentage: Value ÷ (Percentage ÷ 100)

Examples of Percentage Calculations

1. What is 25% of 240?

    25% of 240 = (25 ÷ 100) × 240 = 0.25 × 240 = 60

2. 15 is what percent of 75?

    (15 ÷ 75) × 100 = 0.2 × 100 = 20%

3. A phone was ₹15,000 and now it’s ₹12,000. What’s the percentage decrease?

    (15,000 - 12,000) ÷ 15,000 × 100 = 3,000 ÷ 15,000 × 100 = 20%

4. A population increased from 50,000 to 65,000. What's the percentage increase?

    (65,000 - 50,000) ÷ 50,000 × 100 = 15,000 ÷ 50,000 × 100 = 30%

Types of Percentage Calculations

1. Finding the Percentage of a Value

E.g., What is 18% of 350?

    18/100 × 350 = 63

2. Finding What Percent One Number Is of Another

E.g., What percent is 50 of 200?

    (50 ÷ 200) × 100 = 25%

3. Percentage Increase/Decrease

E.g., Increase from ₹800 to ₹1,000

    (1,000 - 800) ÷ 800 × 100 = 25% increase

E.g., Decrease from 500 to 350

    (500 - 350) ÷ 500 × 100 = 30% decrease

Percentage Change in Real Life

  • Salary Hike: If your salary goes from ₹25,000 to ₹30,000, the percentage increase is (5,000 ÷ 25,000) × 100 = 20%.
  • Price Drop: An item drops from ₹1000 to ₹800. The drop is (200 ÷ 1000) × 100 = 20%.
  • GST Calculation: If an item costs ₹1,200 and GST is 18%, the total cost = ₹1,200 + (18/100 × ₹1,200) = ₹1,416.

Tips for Fast Percentage Calculations

  • 10% of any number: Just move the decimal point one place left (e.g., 10% of 450 = 45)
  • 5%: Half of 10%
  • 20%: Double the value of 10%
  • 25%: One fourth of the total value
  • 50%: Half the value
  • 75%: Three fourths of the total

Conversions Between Decimals and Percentages

  • Decimal → Percentage: Multiply by 100
  • Percentage → Decimal: Divide by 100

Examples:

    0.75 → 75%
    1.25 → 125%
    0.08 → 8%
    25% → 0.25
    100% → 1.0
    150% → 1.5

Reverse Percentage Problems

Q: If 20% of a number is 60, what is the number?

    Let the number be X.
    20% of X = 60 → (20/100) × X = 60 → X = 60 ÷ 0.2 = 300

Advanced Percentage Applications

While basic percentage operations are foundational, percentages are also used in more complex real-world and academic problems. Here are some advanced uses of percentage calculations:

1. Compound Percentage Change

When a quantity changes multiple times by different percentages, the total percentage change is not a simple addition. It’s calculated using multiplication of change factors.

Example: If a product’s price increases by 20% in year 1 and then decreases by 10% in year 2:

      Year 1: 100 → 100 + 20% = 120
      Year 2: 120 → 120 - 10% = 108
      Overall change: (108 - 100)/100 × 100 = 8%

2. Successive Discounts

Successive percentage discounts are common in retail. A 30% discount followed by 20% on the reduced price is not equal to 50% off.

Example: Item price: ₹1,000 → 30% off = ₹700 → 20% off of ₹700 = ₹560

Total discount = ₹1,000 - ₹560 = ₹440 → 44% overall

3. Percentage of Percentage

This occurs in taxation or commission scenarios. If a salesperson earns 10% commission and is taxed 20% on that, the net earning is 80% of 10%.

Example: Sale = ₹10,000 → Commission = ₹1,000 → After 20% tax = ₹800

Net = 80% of 10% = 8%

4. Percentage Allocation

Used in budgeting and expense tracking.

Example: Monthly income = ₹50,000
Rent = 30% → ₹15,000
Groceries = 20% → ₹10,000
Savings = 25% → ₹12,500
Remaining = 25% → ₹12,500

Percentage in Financial Planning

Percentages are essential in finance for evaluating returns, calculating interest, analyzing risk, and making investment decisions. Common financial formulas using percentages include:

  • Return on Investment (ROI): (Profit ÷ Cost) × 100
  • Profit Margin: (Profit ÷ Revenue) × 100
  • Loan Interest: (Rate × Principal × Time) ÷ 100
  • Inflation Rate: ((Current - Previous) ÷ Previous) × 100
  • Depreciation Rate: ((Cost - Salvage) ÷ Cost) × 100

Example: Investment of ₹50,000 returns ₹60,000 in 1 year. ROI = (10,000 ÷ 50,000) × 100 = 20%

Percentage in Academics and Grading

In schools and universities, marks and grades are often based on percentages:

  • Below 35% → Fail
  • 35% – 49% → Pass
  • 50% – 59% → Second Class
  • 60% – 74% → First Class
  • 75% and above → Distinction

Example: Scored 420 out of 600

      (420 ÷ 600) × 100 = 70% → First Class

Percent Error in Science

In experiments and measurements, percent error tells you how close your result is to the true value:

      Percent Error = |Measured - Actual| ÷ Actual × 100

Example: Actual weight = 52g, Measured = 50g

      (2 ÷ 52) × 100 ≈ 3.85%

FAQs – Percentage Calculator

🔹 What is the fastest way to calculate percentages?

Use the formula: (Part ÷ Whole) × 100. For quick estimates, use round numbers or common fractions like 1/2 = 50%, 1/4 = 25%, etc.

🔹 Can I calculate percentage increase/decrease with this calculator?

Yes. Just input the original and new values, and it will compute the percentage change instantly.

🔹 How do I find what number X% is of Y?

Use the formula: (X ÷ 100) × Y

🔹 Is this percentage calculator suitable for exams?

Absolutely. It's perfect for students needing quick checks during revision or homework practice.

🔹 Can this be used in business or tax work?

Yes. Businesses use percentages for profit, tax, discount, commission, pricing, and cost analysis.

🔹 What’s the difference between a percentage and a percentile?

A percentage shows a proportion out of 100. A percentile ranks a value relative to others in a dataset. If you're in the 90th percentile, you've scored better than 90% of others.
