What Is Frequency?

Frequency describes how often a periodic event repeats per unit time. In physics and engineering, frequency is fundamental to waves and oscillations—acoustic, electromagnetic, and electronic signals all rely on precise frequency measurements.

Frequency Units: From Hertz to Megahertz

The SI unit of frequency is the hertz (Hz), defined as one cycle per second. Higher magnitudes use metric prefixes:

  • kHz — kilohertz (10³ Hz)
  • MHz — megahertz (10⁶ Hz)
  • GHz — gigahertz (10⁹ Hz)
  • THz — terahertz (10¹² Hz)

Why Convert MHz to Hz?

Many engineering calculations, simulations, and software tools require input in hertz. Converting megahertz to hertz ensures compatibility, precision, and prevents scaling errors in design parameters.

Common Scenarios
  • RF amplifier gain calculations
  • Filter design and cutoff frequency specification
  • Spectrum analyzer readings
  • Digital signal processor (DSP) sample rate configuration
Formula for Manual Conversion

The conversion formula is straightforward:

Hz = MHz × 10⁶

For example, 100 MHz = 100 × 1,000,000 = 100,000,000 Hz.

Relationship Between Frequency and Period

Frequency (f) and period (T) are inversely related:

T = 1 / f

When f is in hertz, T is in seconds. For example, a 100 MHz signal has a period:

T = 1 / 100 × 10⁶ = 10 ns

Calculating Period from Converted Frequency

After converting MHz to Hz, compute:

  • Period (s) = 1 ÷ result (Hz)
  • Subdivide into nanoseconds (×10⁹) or microseconds (×10⁶) for readability

Example: GPS L1 Signal

GPS L1 carrier at 1575.42 MHz → 1 575 420 000 Hz → period ≈ 634.9 ps.

Practical Use in Timing Circuits

Oscillator periods determine sampling intervals for ADCs and timing resolution in digital counters.

Sampling Theorem & Nyquist Frequency

In digital signal processing, the Nyquist frequency is half the sample rate. Converting MHz to Hz is crucial:

fNyquist = fs / 2

For a 200 MHz ADC, sample rate = 200 MS/s = 200 × 10⁶ Hz → Nyquist = 100 × 10⁶ Hz.

Anti-Aliasing Filter Design

Design low-pass filters with cutoff just above Nyquist; accurate Hz values ensure correct component selection.

Example Filter Calculation

Cutoff = 1.1 × Nyquist = 110 MHz → 110 × 10⁶ Hz. Compute reactance at this frequency for RLC design.

Component Tolerances

Inductor and capacitor tolerances shift cutoff; use precise Hz value to calculate worst-case boundaries.

EMI Considerations

Radio-frequency interference filters require accurate cutoff in Hz to block unwanted harmonics.
