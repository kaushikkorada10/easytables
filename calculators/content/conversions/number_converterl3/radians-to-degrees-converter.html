What Are Radians and Degrees?

Radians and degrees are two units used to measure angles:

  • Degree (°): A full circle is 360 degrees. It’s the most common angle unit in geometry and everyday applications.
  • Radian (rad): A full circle is 2π radians. It’s the standard unit in higher mathematics, calculus, and physics.

The two units are related through the mathematical constant π (pi ≈ 3.14159).

Radians to Degrees Formula

The conversion formula from radians to degrees is straightforward:

Degrees = Radians × (180 / π)

Example: Convert π/4 radians to degrees:

Degrees = (π/4) × (180 / π) = 180 / 4 = 45°

How to Convert in Excel, Python & JavaScript

In Excel: Use the built-in DEGREES() function:

=DEGREES(PI()/4) → 45

In Python:


import math
degrees = math.degrees(math.pi / 4)  # Output: 45.0

In JavaScript:


const radians = Math.PI / 4;
const degrees = radians * (180 / Math.PI); // 45

Radians and the Unit Circle

Radians are deeply tied to the concept of the unit circle in trigonometry. A unit circle is a circle with a radius of 1, centered at the origin (0,0). In this context:

  • 0 radians is at (1, 0)
  • π/2 radians is at (0, 1)
  • π radians is at (–1, 0)
  • 3π/2 radians is at (0, –1)
  • 2π radians brings you back to (1, 0)

Understanding this circular model helps visualize angles and explains why radians are the natural choice in calculus and periodic functions.

Radians in Calculus and Derivatives

Radians simplify mathematical operations. For example, when differentiating trigonometric functions:

  • d/dx [sin(x)] = cos(x) only when x is in radians
  • If x is in degrees, you'd need to apply a conversion factor: d/dx [sin(x°)] = cos(x°) × π/180

Hence, calculus assumes radian measure by default — and radians are the preferred angular unit in all advanced math contexts.

Historical Context: Where Did Radians Come From?

The concept of the radian was introduced in the 18th century. The term “radian” was first used by James Thomson in 1873. It originates from the idea that an angle is formed by the length of the arc it subtends on a unit circle.

Since the circumference of a unit circle is 2π, that full angle is 2π radians — making 1 radian the angle where the arc length equals the radius.

Radians in Circular Motion and Dynamics

In physics, radians play a central role in describing rotational motion. Common formulas that rely on radians include:

  • Angular velocity (ω): ω = θ / t (θ must be in radians)
  • Angular acceleration (α): α = Δω / Δt
  • Arc length: s = r × θ, where θ is in radians
  • Centripetal acceleration: a = r × ω²

In all these equations, if θ were expressed in degrees, the math would break unless additional conversion factors are introduced. That’s why radians are universally used in rotational dynamics and kinematics.

Radians in Trigonometric Identities

Radians preserve the elegance of trigonometric identities. For example:

lim (x → 0) [sin(x)/x] = 1

This limit is only true if x is in radians. If x were in degrees, the result would be π / 180, distorting calculus outcomes and derivatives. Hence, radians are the default in all advanced mathematics.

Use of Degrees in Navigation & Geography

While radians dominate in calculus and engineering, degrees remain essential in navigation, mapping, and GPS technology:

  • Latitude and longitude: Always measured in degrees
  • Compass directions: 0° (North), 90° (East), 180° (South), 270° (West)
  • Angular bearings: Used in aviation and marine navigation

Therefore, converters help bridge these two domains — mathematics (radians) and geography (degrees).

Radians and Taylor Series Expansions

Functions like sine, cosine, and tangent are often expressed using Taylor Series for approximation. These expansions only work when the angle is in radians:


sin(x) = x − x³/3! + x⁵/5! − x⁷/7! + ...
cos(x) = 1 − x²/2! + x⁴/4! − x⁶/6! + ...

If x were in degrees, the series wouldn’t converge correctly. This is another reason radians are preferred in computational mathematics.

Radians in Electrical Engineering

In AC (alternating current) circuits, phase angles and signal frequencies are typically measured in radians:

  • Sinusoidal wave function: V(t) = Vmax × sin(ωt + φ)
  • ω (angular frequency): measured in rad/s
  • φ (phase shift): often in radians for easier integration and differentiation

When plotting or analyzing signals, engineers may convert radians into degrees for more intuitive understanding or to match oscilloscope settings.
