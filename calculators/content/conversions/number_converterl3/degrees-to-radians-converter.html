What Are Degrees?

Degrees (°) are a standard unit of measuring angles. A full circle has 360 degrees, with each degree representing 1/360th of a full rotation. Degrees are commonly used in geometry, navigation, surveying, and everyday life.

What Are Radians?

Radians are the SI (International System) unit for measuring angles based on arc length. A full circle is 2π radians, making radians ideal for use in higher mathematics, especially calculus and trigonometry.

In a circle, 1 radian is the angle created when the arc length equals the radius of the circle. Radians are used extensively in:

  • Calculus and trigonometric functions
  • Physics equations for motion and waves
  • Graphics programming (rotations, animation)
  • Signal processing and robotics

Degrees to Radians Conversion Formula

The formula to convert degrees to radians is:

radians = degrees × (π ÷ 180)

Example:

  • Convert 90° to radians: 90 × (π ÷ 180) = π/2 ≈ 1.5708
  • Convert 180° to radians: 180 × (π ÷ 180) = π ≈ 3.1416
  • Convert 360° to radians: 360 × (π ÷ 180) = 2π ≈ 6.2832

Radians in Calculus and Trigonometry

In calculus, functions like sin(x), cos(x), and tan(x) are defined using radians for smooth differentiation and integration:

  • Limit identity: lim(x→0) sin(x)/x = 1 is true only if x is in radians
  • Derivatives: d/dx [sin(x)] = cos(x), valid only when x is in radians
  • Integration: ∫ sin(x) dx = -cos(x) + C, assuming x is in radians

Thus, converting to radians ensures mathematical correctness when applying calculus rules.
