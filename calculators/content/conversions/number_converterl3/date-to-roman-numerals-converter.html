What Are Roman Numerals?

Roman numerals are a number system developed in ancient Rome, using combinations of letters from the Latin alphabet: I, V, X, L, C, D, M. They were commonly used across Europe for centuries, especially in manuscripts, clocks, titles, and monumental inscriptions.

For example:

  • 1 = I
  • 5 = V
  • 10 = X
  • 50 = L
  • 100 = C
  • 500 = D
  • 1000 = M

Roman Calendar Months and Their Origins

The names of our modern months have Roman roots. You can enhance your converted dates with classical names:

Advanced Conversion Logic (Behind the Scenes)

Here’s how the logic works programmatically:

  1. The input date is split into three numeric values: day, month, and year
  2. Each number is processed using a Roman numeral algorithm based on digit place value:
    • Thousands (M)
    • Hundreds (C, D, M)
    • Tens (X, L, C)
    • Units (I, V, X)
  3. The three results are joined with a delimiter (like a dot or dash)

The process ensures leading zeros are ignored and the correct subtractive forms like IV and IX are used.

Common Mistakes When Writing Roman Numerals

  • Using IIII instead of IV for 4 — although IIII is valid on clock faces
  • Writing IC for 99 (wrong); correct is XCIX
  • Placing subtractive symbols in the wrong order (e.g., IIV is invalid)
  • Using lower case (e.g., mmxxv) where upper case is typically standard
