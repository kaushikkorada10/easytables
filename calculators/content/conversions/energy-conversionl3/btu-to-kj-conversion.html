What Is a BTU?

A BTU, or British Thermal Unit, is a non-SI unit of heat defined as the amount of energy required to raise the temperature of one pound (approximately 0.4536 kg) of water by one degree Fahrenheit at standard atmospheric pressure. Though modern science prefers SI units, BTUs remain common in HVAC (heating, ventilation, and air conditioning), refrigeration, boiler ratings, and energy billing.

Origin and Usage

Introduced during the Industrial Revolution in Britain, BTUs gained widespread use in English-speaking countries for heat measurement in furnaces, boilers, and air conditioners.

Common Ratings

You’ll encounter terms like “12,000 BTU air conditioner” or “100,000 BTU furnace,” indicating the heat removal or generation capacity.

Imperial Legacy

Since BTU isn’t part of the International System of Units (SI), conversion to Joules or kilojoules is crucial for scientific research, global engineering projects, and data interchange.

What Is a Kilojoule (kJ)?

A Joule (J) is the SI unit of energy, defined as the work done when a force of one newton moves an object one meter. A kilojoule (kJ) equals 1,000 J. Kilojoules are ubiquitous in scientific disciplines, engineering analyses, nutrition labeling (e.g., food energy), and renewable energy reports.

SI Standard

Universally recognized, facilitating data sharing and compliance with international standards.

Scalability

Kilojoules bridge the gap between small Joule quantities (e.g., 500 J) and large megajoule scales (e.g., 2 MJ = 2,000 kJ).

Applications

Chemical reaction energies, battery capacities, calorimetry, and thermal efficiency calculations.

BTU to kJ Conversion Factor

The precise conversion factor between BTU and kilojoules is derived from the BTU-to-Joule relationship:

Exact Conversion

1 BTU = 1,055.05585 J

Convert to kJ

1 BTU = 1,055.05585 J ÷ 1,000 = 1.05505585 kJ
Rounded Factor
1 BTU ≈ 1.05506 kJ
Conversion Formulas
kilojoules (kJ) = BTU × 1.05505585  
BTU = kilojoules (kJ) ÷ 1.05505585

Step-by-Step BTU to kJ Conversion Guide

  1. Determine Your BTU Value

    Identify the heat rating or measurement in BTUs. Labels on HVAC equipment or data from calorimeters provide this value.

  2. Multiply by the Conversion Factor

    Use 1.05505585 kJ per BTU. For example:

    8,500 BTU × 1.05505585 kJ/BTU = 8,967.0 kJ

  3. Round Appropriately

    For engineering purposes, round to two decimal places (e.g., 8,967.97 kJ); for quick estimates, one decimal place may suffice.

  4. Verify Units

    Ensure your result clearly indicates kilojoules (kJ).

  5. Document Calculations

    Record both the formula and conversion factor in reports or logs to maintain traceability.

Common Conversion Examples

Example 1: Air Conditioner Capacity

A 12,000 BTU/h air conditioner’s hourly cooling capacity expressed in kJ: 

12,000 BTU × 1.05505585 kJ/BTU = 12,660.67 kJ
Equivalent to 3.52 kW (1 kW = 3.6 kJ/s).

Example 2: Furnace Output

A furnace releasing 150,000 BTU per cycle: 

150,000 BTU × 1.05505585 kJ/BTU = 158,258.38 kJ  
158,258.38 kJ ÷ 1,000 = 158.26 MJ

Example 3: Calorimetry Experiment

In a chemistry lab, burning a sample yields 250 BTU of heat: 

250 BTU × 1.05505585 kJ/BTU = 263.76 kJ
Lab reports and publications use this SI-compliant value for consistency.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

1. Why convert BTU to kJ instead of Joules?

Kilojoules provide a more practical scale for large energy quantities. While Joules offer fine granularity, kJ avoids long numbers and scientific notation.

2. Is it acceptable to use 1.055 kJ per BTU for estimates?

Yes, for rough calculations, 1.055 kJ/BTU (rounded) yields an error under 0.01%. Use the full factor for high-precision work.

3. How do I convert a BTU/h (per hour) rating to kJ/s (kW)?

Divide the kJ value by 3,600 s:
kJ/h ÷ 3,600 = kJ/s = kW
Example: 12,660.67 kJ/h ÷ 3,600 = 3.517 kW.

4. What other thermal units are common?

Calorie (cal), kilocalorie (kcal), therm, foot-pound (ft·lb), electronvolt (eV). Chain conversions as needed.
