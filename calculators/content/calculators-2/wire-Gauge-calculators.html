What Is a Wire Gauge?

Wire gauge refers to the diameter of an electrical wire. It impacts how much current a wire can handle, how much resistance it offers, and how much voltage drop may occur over a given length. Smaller gauge numbers represent thicker wires, while higher numbers represent thinner wires.

For example:

  • AWG 4 is thicker than AWG 12
  • AWG 20 is thinner than AWG 10

Understanding the Physics Behind Wire Gauge

Wire gauge directly impacts the electrical properties of a circuit. Thicker wires (lower gauge numbers) offer lower resistance, which allows more current to pass through without generating excess heat. On the other hand, thinner wires (higher gauge numbers) restrict the flow of current, increasing resistance and heat generation over long distances.

This is explained by Ohmâ€™s Law: V = IR, where:

  • V is voltage
  • I is current
  • R is resistance

When resistance increases due to the use of a small wire gauge, it can result in voltage drop and reduced efficiency of the connected devices. This is why matching the right wire gauge with the expected current load and length is crucial in system design.
