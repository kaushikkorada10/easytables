üî¢ What is an Average?

An **average** is a single value that represents the central or typical value of a data set. In statistics, the most commonly used average is the **arithmetic mean**, which is calculated by adding up all the numbers and dividing the sum by the count of numbers.

For example:
(10 + 20 + 30) / 3 = 60 / 3 = 20

üìò Types of Averages

  • Mean (Arithmetic Average): Total sum of values divided by the number of values
  • Median: The middle number when data is sorted
  • Mode: The number that appears most frequently in the list
  • Weighted Average: Each value is multiplied by a weight, then divided by the total weights

üìà Differences Between Mean, Median, and Mode

While all three are types of averages, they serve different purposes:

  • Mean is useful for evenly distributed data. It‚Äôs sensitive to outliers and extreme values.
  • Median is better for skewed data or distributions with extreme highs/lows, as it focuses on the middle value.
  • Mode identifies the most common value and is ideal for analyzing frequency or popularity.

For example, if you're calculating average income, the mean may be skewed by billionaires. In such cases, the median income gives a better picture of what most people earn.

üìè When to Use Weighted Average

Weighted averages are used when certain values in the data set are more important than others. This is especially helpful in:

  • Calculating GPA, where credit hours differ between courses
  • Portfolio analysis in finance, where each stock has a different investment size
  • Performance reviews where tasks are weighted based on importance

Example: A student scores 90 in a 3-credit course and 80 in a 2-credit course. Weighted average:

üßæ Special Cases and Edge Conditions

Some things to watch out for when calculating averages:

  • Empty Input: If no numbers are entered, average is undefined.
  • Single Entry: The average is the number itself.
  • All Zeros: Average is zero, but this may indicate an error in data collection.
  • Outliers: One large or small number can distort the mean; consider checking the median.
