What Is a Weighted Average?

A weighted average is an average that takes into account the relative importance or frequency (called weights) of each value. Unlike a simple mean where all values contribute equally, a weighted mean reflects the significance of each data point.

Weighted Average Formula:

Weighted Average = (∑ wi * xi) / (∑ wi)

  • wi = weight of the ith data point
  • xi = value of the ith data point

Example

Suppose you have three test scores: 70, 80, and 90 with weights of 1, 2, and 3 respectively.

Weighted Average = (70×1 + 80×2 + 90×3) / (1 + 2 + 3) = (70 + 160 + 270) / 6 = 500 / 6 = 83.33

Step-by-Step Example with Explanations

Let’s go deeper into how the calculator works by walking through a complete example.

Scenario: A student has three subjects with the following marks and corresponding weights:

  • Math – 95 marks (Weight: 4)
  • Science – 85 marks (Weight: 3)
  • History – 75 marks (Weight: 2)

Step 1: Multiply each score by its weight:

  • Math: 95 × 4 = 380
  • Science: 85 × 3 = 255
  • History: 75 × 2 = 150

Step 2: Add all weighted values: 380 + 255 + 150 = 785

Step 3: Add all weights: 4 + 3 + 2 = 9

Step 4: Divide weighted sum by total weights: 785 / 9 = 87.22

So, the weighted average score is 87.22.

Understanding the Importance of Weights

The idea behind a weighted average is to better reflect the importance of certain values over others. For example:

  • In academic grading, final exams often carry more weight than class tests.
  • In budgeting, larger expenses should have a greater influence than minor purchases.
  • In statistical analysis, survey responses from underrepresented groups may be given more weight.

Weighted Average in Statistics and Data Science

Weighted averages are often used in:

  • Weighted regression: Giving more importance to reliable observations in a dataset.
  • Bias correction: Adjusting results to balance data imbalance.
  • Performance metrics: Computing scores across imbalanced classes (e.g., in precision-recall, F1-score, etc.).

Weighted Moving Averages

A variation used in time-series analysis is the weighted moving average (WMA). Unlike simple moving averages, WMA assigns decreasing weights to older data points, which is useful for stock price predictions, trend forecasting, and monitoring production output.

Academic Applications

Professors and students frequently use weighted averages to compute grades based on different components:

  • Homework: 20%
  • Midterms: 30%
  • Final Exam: 50%

If a student scores 90 on homework, 80 on midterms, and 70 on finals, the weighted average would be:
(90×0.2 + 80×0.3 + 70×0.5) = 18 + 24 + 35 = 77

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Entering mismatched weights and values
  • Using percentage weights without converting them to decimal form when needed
  • Assuming weights are the same when they are not
  • Forgetting to normalize weights (they don’t need to sum to 1, but must be proportionally accurate)

Advanced Weighted Average Concepts

  • Geometric weighted averages: Used in financial indices when compounding returns.
  • Harmonic weighted averages: Useful in rate-based calculations such as speed and efficiency.
  • Inverse weighting: Smaller values have more weight; common in reliability analysis.
