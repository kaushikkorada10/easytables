What Is Trigonometry?

Trigonometry is a branch of mathematics that studies the relationships between angles and sides of triangles, particularly right-angled triangles. The six primary trigonometric functions are:

  • sin(θ) = opposite / hypotenuse
  • cos(θ) = adjacent / hypotenuse
  • tan(θ) = opposite / adjacent
  • csc(θ) = 1 / sin(θ)
  • sec(θ) = 1 / cos(θ)
  • cot(θ) = 1 / tan(θ)

Graphical Interpretation of Trig Functions

Each trig function has its own graph, which can help visualize periodicity, amplitude, and asymptotes. For example, sine and cosine have a wave-like pattern, while tangent has vertical asymptotes and repeats every 180° (π radians).

Trigonometric Identities

  • Pythagorean Identity: sin²(θ) + cos²(θ) = 1
  • Quotient Identities: tan(θ) = sin(θ)/cos(θ), cot(θ) = cos(θ)/sin(θ)
  • Reciprocal Identities: csc(θ) = 1/sin(θ), sec(θ) = 1/cos(θ), cot(θ) = 1/tan(θ)

Applications of Trigonometry

  • Engineering: Structural analysis, wave dynamics, electronics
  • Architecture: Calculating angles in design and elevation
  • Physics: Describing oscillations, projectiles, and vectors
  • Astronomy: Measuring distances and angles of celestial objects
  • Navigation: Calculating course corrections and bearings

Using Trigonometry in the Real World

Trigonometric functions are not just abstract math concepts — they are actively used in everyday life and professional industries. Here are some examples:

  • Flight navigation: Pilots use trig functions to calculate wind correction angles and altitudes.
  • Surveying: Trigonometry is fundamental for determining land area, slope, and elevation.
  • Music and sound engineering: Waveforms and harmonics rely heavily on sine and cosine functions.
  • Computer graphics: 3D rendering engines use trigonometry to simulate realistic movement and lighting.

Solving Triangles with Trigonometry

In addition to finding function values, this calculator can assist in solving full triangle problems using the following principles:

  • SOH-CAH-TOA: A popular mnemonic to remember the definitions of sin, cos, and tan.
  • Law of Sines: sin(A)/a = sin(B)/b = sin(C)/c
  • Law of Cosines: c² = a² + b² - 2ab·cos(C)

These laws are especially useful in non-right-angled triangle calculations, commonly found in real-world geometry problems.

Inverse Trigonometric Functions

The calculator can also support inverse functions, which help you find angles when you know the ratio:

  • arcsin(x): Gives angle whose sine is x
  • arccos(x): Gives angle whose cosine is x
  • arctan(x): Gives angle whose tangent is x

This is helpful when working backward from measurements to find angles, such as in navigation or construction.

Unit Circle Reference

All trig functions are deeply connected to the unit circle, a circle with a radius of 1 centered at the origin of a coordinate plane. The unit circle helps define function values for all angles and is a critical tool for understanding periodicity and signs of trigonometric functions across quadrants.

Angle Conversions

Sometimes your values may be in radians, but you need them in degrees — or vice versa. Here are handy conversions:

  • Radians to degrees: Multiply by 180/π
  • Degrees to radians: Multiply by π/180

Example: π/6 × 180/π = 30°

Advanced Users: Combining Functions

Advanced users may need to work with composite functions like:

  • sin(2θ) = 2sin(θ)cos(θ)
  • cos(2θ) = cos²(θ) - sin²(θ)
  • tan(2θ) = 2tan(θ)/(1 - tan²(θ))

These identities are useful in calculus, signal processing, and physics. While the current calculator handles basic functions, you can break these into parts and calculate each segment easily.
