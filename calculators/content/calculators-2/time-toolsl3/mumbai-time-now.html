Historical Context

IST was officially adopted on January 1, 1906, using the 82°30′ E meridian (near Allahabad) as a reference. Mumbai previously followed Bombay Time (UTC +4:51) until IST was fully implemented across India by 1955 :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}.

Why Does India Use UTC +5:30?

India spans a wide longitude, and the half-hour offset allows the IST to represent a central average to balance sunrise and sunset times across the country :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}. It avoids extremes that would affect quality of life in eastern and western regions.

Is There a Push to Adopt Dual Time Zones in India?

Due to India's vast east-west spread, there have been proposals to introduce a second time zone (IST-II) for northeastern states. However, Mumbai and western India will continue to follow IST (UTC +5:30). The one-nation-one-time policy ensures simplicity, even though cities like Mumbai and Kohima experience very different sunrises.

For now, IST remains India’s only official time standard, and Mumbai is one of its most punctual representatives of economic and cultural timing.
