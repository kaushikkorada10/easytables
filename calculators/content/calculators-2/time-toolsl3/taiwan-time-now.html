Brief History of Taiwan Standard Time

  • 1896 – 1905: Under Japanese rule Taiwan used UTC+9 (“Japan Standard Time”).
  • 1905 – 1937: Switched to UTC+8 when the 105° E meridian became the legal zone for the entire island :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}.
  • 1946 – 1979: Taiwan observed Daylight Saving Time in summer for 19 different seasons – but dropped DST permanently after September 30 1979 :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}.
  • 1980 – present: Taiwan uses a single, year-round UTC+8, now colloquially branded Taipei Time, Taiwan Time or Taiwan Standard Time.

Who Governs Time in Taiwan?

The **Bureau of Standards, Metrology and Inspection (BSMI)** publishes legal time bulletins, while the National Communications Commission (NCC) disseminates it via telecom networks. Banks, airports and power utilities must calibrate their systems to BSMI’s master clock under national law :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}.

🕰 Taiwan Time in Global Standards & Atomic Precision

The official Taiwan Standard Time (TST) is managed by BSMI through the National Standard Time & Frequency Laboratory, which sources atomic clocks synchronized with the International Bureau of Weights and Measures (BIPM). These caesium clocks maintain legal time, broadcasting via NTP, radio, and satellite links—ensuring TST remains accurate to better than one millisecond globally :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}.

Since 2020, BSMI has implemented upgrades to optical frequency standards similar to KRISS and NIST. These next-gen atomic clocks operate with accuracy up to 2×10–16—equivalent to losing just one second over billions of years :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}. This places Taiwan among the most precisely tracked time zones worldwide.

Time Zone Path: Taiwan’s Shift from UTC+9 to UTC+8

  • 1896–1905: Adopted Japan Standard Time (UTC+9) under Qing control.
  • 1905–1937: Shifted to Greenwich Mean Time +8 (UTC+8).
