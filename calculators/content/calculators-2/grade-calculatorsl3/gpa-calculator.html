What Is GPA?

GPA stands for Grade Point Average. It’s a standardized measure of academic performance that converts letter grades into numerical values and averages them over a term or cumulative period. GPA is used by schools, colleges, and universities worldwide to assess student achievement and eligibility for honors, scholarships, and program admission.

Unweighted vs. Weighted GPA

There are two primary GPA scales:

  • Unweighted GPA: Uses a standard 4.0 scale. Every course counts equally, regardless of difficulty (A=4.0, B=3.0, C=2.0, D=1.0, F=0).
  • Weighted GPA: Accounts for course rigor (honors, AP, IB). Letter grades in advanced courses earn extra points (e.g., A=5.0 in AP courses).

Why Use Weighted GPA?

Weighted GPA rewards students who take challenging coursework. It differentiates between straight-A students in standard vs. advanced classes, providing a more nuanced view of academic effort and capability.

Standard 4.0 Scale Conversion

Most institutions convert letter grades to grade points as follows:

  • A = 4.0
  • A- = 3.7
  • B+ = 3.3
  • B = 3.0
  • B- = 2.7
  • C+ = 2.3
  • C = 2.0
  • C- = 1.7
  • D+ = 1.3
  • D = 1.0
  • D- = 0.7
  • F = 0
Alternate Scales

Some schools use a 5.0 scale or percentage-to-GPA charts. Always check your institution’s policy before calculating.

How to Calculate GPA Manually

Computing GPA involves two main steps: converting grades to grade points and averaging those points, weighted by course credit hours.

Step 1: Convert Letter Grades to Grade Points

Use your school’s conversion chart to map each letter grade to its numeric equivalent. Write down the grade point for each course.

Step 2: Multiply by Credit Hours

For each course, multiply the grade point by the credit hours (or units) assigned to that course. This gives you the quality points for each class.

Example Calculation

Consider a semester with the following courses:

  • Calculus I: A (4.0) × 4 credits = 16.0 quality points
  • English Lit: B+ (3.3) × 3 credits = 9.9 quality points
  • Biology: B (3.0) × 4 credits = 12.0 quality points
  • History: A- (3.7) × 3 credits = 11.1 quality points

Total Quality Points = 16.0 + 9.9 + 12.0 + 11.1 = 49.0
Total Credits = 4 + 3 + 4 + 3 = 14

GPA = Total Quality Points ÷ Total Credits = 49.0 ÷ 14 = 3.5

Calculating Cumulative GPA

To compute a cumulative GPA across multiple terms, sum all quality points and all credits for all terms, then divide. Do not average individual term GPAs.

Rounding Policies

Institutions vary on rounding: some round to the nearest hundredth, others truncate. Check your school’s rules before reporting your GPA.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Q: Can I include audit courses?

A: Audited courses typically do not count toward GPA; deselect them in settings to exclude.

Q: How do I handle incompletes?

A: Incompletes count as “I” until resolved. Enter the final grade once posted to get an accurate GPA.

Q: What if my school uses a plus/minus system?

A: Select the plus/minus grading option to enable precise conversion (e.g., B+ = 3.3).

Q: How often should I recalculate?

A: Update after each grading event (midterm, finals) to track progress and identify courses needing extra attention.

Q: Is my GPA rounded?

A: Most institutions round to two decimal places. Adjust rounding preferences in calculator settings.

Common GPA Calculation Pitfalls

1. Omitting Courses: Failing to include required courses—especially pass/fail inscriptions—can skew averages. Always verify included course lists.

2. Incorrect Scale Mapping: Mixing different grading scales (e.g., mapping a UK 70% to a US 3.0) leads to errors. Use the built-in international converter to standardize scales first.

3. Rounding Too Early: Rounding intermediate quality point totals instead of final GPA can introduce small but noticeable differences. Carry full precision until the last step.
