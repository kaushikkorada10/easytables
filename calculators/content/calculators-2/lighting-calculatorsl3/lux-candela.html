Understanding Lux and Candela

Lux (lx) is a unit of illuminance. It measures how much luminous flux is spread over a given area. One lux is equal to one lumen per square meter. Lux tells you how brightly an area is lit.

Candela (cd) is the SI base unit of luminous intensity. It represents how much light is emitted in a particular direction. It’s commonly used for measuring focused light sources like LEDs, laser beams, and flashlights.

Why Convert Lux to Candela?

Converting lux to candela helps in:

  • Lighting design and simulations
  • Projector and stage lighting calculations
  • Photometric analysis
  • Spotlight and beam angle optimization
  • Safety light evaluations in aviation, marine, and emergency systems

Lux to Candela Formula

The mathematical formula to convert lux to candela depends on the distance from the light source:

Candela = Lux × (Distance)2

Where:

  • Lux is the illuminance measured on a surface (in lux)
  • Distance is the distance between the light source and the surface (in meters)

Example:

If the illuminance is 100 lux at 2 meters from a light source:

Candela = 100 × (2)2 = 100 × 4 = 400 cd

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

Q: Can I convert lux to candela without distance?

No. The conversion requires distance in meters from the light source to the illuminated surface. Without that, you can’t accurately determine candela.

Q: What’s the difference between lumens, lux, and candela?

  • Lumens: Total light emitted
  • Lux: Light received per square meter
  • Candela: Light intensity in a particular direction

Q: Does beam angle affect the conversion?

Yes. For more accurate real-world calculations, the beam angle of the light source should be considered, especially when designing spotlights or directional lighting. However, for basic lux-to-candela conversion, only the lux and distance are needed.

Factors Affecting Lux to Candela Conversion

It’s important to note that several factors can affect your lux to candela calculations:

  • Distance Accuracy: Small errors in measuring distance can lead to large differences due to the square term.
  • Light Source Type: Point sources like LEDs produce more predictable results than broad diffuse sources.
  • Environmental Conditions: Fog, smoke, or reflections can affect lux readings, especially outdoors or in industrial settings.

Optimizing Lighting Based on Candela Output

If your target is to meet specific candela requirements for safety or design purposes, reverse engineering from lux values using this calculator is effective. You can adjust light intensity, beam focus, and fixture placement to achieve desired candela levels.

  • FAA Lighting Requirements: Define candela outputs for runway lights and aircraft navigation beacons.
  • EN 1838: European standard for emergency lighting, specifying minimum candela ratings.
