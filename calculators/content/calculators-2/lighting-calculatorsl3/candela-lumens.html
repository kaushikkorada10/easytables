What Is Candela (cd)?

Candela is the SI unit of luminous intensity. It measures how much light a source emits in a particular direction. If you imagine a laser pointer or flashlight beam, candela defines how intense that beam is in one direction. One candela is roughly the light intensity of a common candle.

What Is Lumen (lm)?

Lumen is the unit of luminous flux, which measures the total amount of visible light emitted by a source in all directions. Unlike candela, which is directional, lumen is about total output. More lumens mean a brighter light overall, regardless of direction.

Candela vs Lumens – What's the Difference?

  • Candela (cd): Light intensity in a specific direction
  • Lumens (lm): Total light output in all directions

Think of candela as a laser pointer and lumens as a light bulb. The laser pointer has a high candela but low lumens, while a light bulb emits high lumens over a wide area but lower candela in any one direction.

Candela to Lumens Formula

The formula to convert candela to lumens depends on the beam angle or solid angle of the light beam:

lumens = candela × solid angle (in steradians)

In a directional beam, the light spreads out in a cone. You must calculate the solid angle in steradians from the beam angle in degrees.

Convert Beam Angle to Solid Angle

For a conical beam:

Ω (steradians) = 2π × (1 - cos(θ / 2))

Where θ is the full beam angle in degrees.

Full Formula

lumens = candela × 2π × (1 - cos(beam_angle / 2))

Limitations of Candela to Lumens Conversion

This conversion assumes uniform light distribution across the beam, which may not be accurate for all light types. Reflectors, lenses, and beam hotspots can skew real-world results. For precise photometry, refer to IES files or manufacturer data.

Candela to Lumens FAQs

Q1: Can I convert candela to lumens without beam angle?

No. You need the beam angle (or solid angle in steradians) to convert accurately.

Q2: How many lumens is 1 candela?

It depends on the beam angle. For example, with a 90° beam, 1 candela ≈ 8.4 lumens.

Q3: What beam angle gives the highest lumens from 1 candela?

A wider beam angle gives more lumens for the same candela value since light is spread out more.

Q4: What’s a typical candela value for a flashlight?

Anywhere from 500 cd (floodlight) to 100,000+ cd (tactical spotlight).

Q5: Do lumens measure brightness?

Yes. Lumens indicate how bright the light appears overall. More lumens = brighter light.

Understanding Steradians and Beam Geometry

To truly understand how candela translates into lumens, we need to dive deeper into the geometry of light. In three-dimensional space, light doesn't just move in straight lines—it spreads out in angles, forming cones. This is where the concept of the steradian becomes important.

A steradian (sr) is the SI unit of a solid angle, much like how radians are used in two dimensions. A full sphere contains exactly 4π steradians. So if you had a perfectly spherical light source emitting equally in all directions with 1 candela of intensity, its total light output would be:

lumens = candela × 4π ≈ candela × 12.57

This only applies to omnidirectional sources. Most modern LED fixtures, flashlights, and architectural lights have directional beams, so we use smaller portions of the 4π solid angle.

Beam Angle and Coverage

Beam angle dramatically affects the number of lumens produced from a candela value. A tighter beam angle focuses light into a smaller area, increasing intensity but reducing overall luminous flux. Conversely, a wide beam spreads light more diffusely, reducing intensity but increasing coverage and total lumens.

Here’s a basic breakdown:

  • 10° beam: highly focused, used in spotlights
  • 30°–60° beam: general-purpose lighting, recessed ceiling lights
  • 90°–120° beam: wide area coverage, LED panels

The beam angle is essential to compute lumens accurately from candela. Without it, the conversion lacks precision.

Using Candela and Lumens in Lighting Design

When designing lighting for commercial or residential spaces, both candela and lumens help determine proper fixture placement and selection. Here’s how:

  • Use lumens to ensure sufficient brightness in a space (e.g., 500 lm/m² in offices)
  • Use candela to control the beam’s direction and intensity (e.g., highlight artwork or signage)

Interior designers often work closely with electrical engineers to balance visual comfort and functional light. For example, track lights with 3000 cd intensity at a 30° beam angle provide ~630 lumens, perfect for spotlighting a feature wall.

Lighting Efficiency and Energy Savings

Understanding candela-to-lumen conversions also plays a role in energy efficiency. Lumen per watt (lm/W) is a measure of lighting efficacy. Knowing how lumens relate to candela allows you to calculate how efficiently a light directs energy:

Efficiency (lm/W) = Total lumens / Power consumption (W)

Modern LED lights boast high efficacy, often 100–150 lm/W, far surpassing old incandescent bulbs (10–15 lm/W). Proper beam control using candelas ensures light goes only where needed, minimizing wastage and improving system performance.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Assuming 1 candela = 1 lumen: Only true at 1 steradian, which is not typical
  • Ignoring beam angle: Essential to find the total light output
  • Forgetting cosine conversion: The cosine of half the beam angle is a key part of the formula
  • Using peak candela: Always use average candela if available, especially for wide or uneven beams

Photometry Reports and Light Planning
