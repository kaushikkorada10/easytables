What Are Lumens?

Lumens (lm) are a unit of luminous flux, representing the total amount of visible light emitted by a light source. It measures the overall brightness, regardless of direction. When you read a product specification like "300 lumens," it refers to the total visible light output in all directions.

What Is Millicandela (mcd)?

Millicandela (mcd) is a unit of luminous intensity. It measures how bright the light appears in a specific direction. One candela (cd) equals 1000 millicandela (mcd). Unlike lumens, which consider the total output, millicandela is ideal for measuring how focused a light is.

This makes millicandela a crucial metric for evaluating LEDs, spotlights, flashlights, and small directional light sources like indicators and remote control IR diodes.

Lumens to Millicandela Conversion Formula

The relationship between lumens and millicandelas depends on the beam angle. Here's the formula to convert lumens to candela first:

cd = lumens / (2π × (1 - cos(θ / 2)))

Then, multiply the result by 1000 to convert to millicandela:

mcd = cd × 1000

Variables:

  • lumens: total light output
  • θ: beam angle in degrees
  • mcd: resulting light intensity in millicandela

Example Calculation

Let’s say you have a 2-lumen LED with a beam angle of 20°:

cd = 2 / (2π × (1 - cos(10°))) ≈ 2 / 0.095 ≈ 21.05 cd

Now, convert to millicandela:

mcd = 21.05 × 1000 = 21,050 mcd

This means that even a 2-lumen LED can have over 20,000 mcd if its beam is very narrow, which is common in status indicators or laser applications.

Applications That Use Millicandela Ratings

1. LED Circuit Design

When building custom lighting circuits, engineers need to know the directional intensity (mcd) for current-limiting resistors and brightness optimization.

2. Automotive and Aviation Signal Lights

Tail lights, dashboard indicators, and cockpit signaling use directional LEDs rated in millicandela to ensure visibility under sunlight or harsh conditions.

3. Infrared and Laser Applications

Remote controls and IR sensors operate using highly focused beams. The mcd rating helps manufacturers understand how intense the beam will appear over long distances.

4. Flashlights and Spot Beams

A flashlight with high lumens but a wide beam may seem less bright than a low-lumen flashlight with a narrow, high-mcd beam. This is critical for tactical and professional lighting tools.

Difference Between Candela and Millicandela

The only difference is the unit scale. 1 candela = 1000 millicandela. Use millicandela when dealing with small, precise light sources like LEDs. Use candela when working with large-scale lighting fixtures such as projectors, car headlights, or searchlights.

Designing for Brightness vs Focus

It’s a common misconception that a light with more lumens is “brighter.” In truth, perceived brightness depends on focus and application:

  • High lumens, low mcd: Good for lighting rooms or diffuse areas (e.g., lanterns)
  • Low lumens, high mcd: Ideal for focused beams or alert indicators (e.g., laser pointers, warning lights)

This is why designers use both lumen and mcd ratings to balance spread and intensity depending on the intended task.

How Reflectors and Optics Influence mcd

Even without changing the lumen value of an LED, its millicandela output can be increased using optical engineering:

  • Collimating lenses: Focus scattered light into narrow beams, increasing intensity
  • Reflective cones: Redirect wide-angle light to a narrower viewing cone
  • Diffusers: Do the opposite, scattering light to reduce directional intensity

By modeling these systems using known lumen outputs, designers can calculate and verify the resulting mcd using this calculator.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Q1: Can I convert millicandela back to lumens?

Yes, but you’ll also need the beam angle. The reverse formula is:

lumens = (mcd / 1000) × 2π × (1 - cos(θ / 2))

Q2: Are millicandela and lux the same?

No. Millicandela measures intensity in a direction, while lux measures illumination on a surface. Both are derived from lumens but serve different applications.

Q3: Can one LED have different mcd values?

Yes, depending on the lens, reflector, or beam angle used. The same LED die may have vastly different mcd ratings based on optical configuration.

Q4: Does a higher mcd always mean better?

Not necessarily. It depends on the application. A diffuse reading light benefits from lower mcd and higher spread, while a status indicator or beam LED benefits from high mcd.
