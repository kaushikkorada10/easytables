Candela to Lux Calculator – Measure Illuminance Accurately

In the world of lighting design, photography, architecture, and engineering, understanding light measurement is essential. Two commonly used units are candela (cd) and lux (lx). While candela represents luminous intensity in a specific direction, lux measures the amount of light (illuminance) received on a surface. Our Candela to Lux Calculator helps you easily convert luminous intensity to surface illumination using distance as the determining factor.

What Is Candela (cd)?

Candela is the SI unit of luminous intensity. It quantifies how bright a light source appears in a specific direction. One candela is roughly equal to the light emitted by a standard candle. A flashlight or laser pointer often has high candela values because they concentrate light in a focused beam.

What Is Lux (lx)?

Lux is the SI unit of illuminance. It measures the amount of light falling on a surface per unit area. One lux equals one lumen per square meter. Lux helps assess how well a space is lit—for example, offices typically require around 300–500 lux for ideal working conditions.

Candela to Lux Conversion Formula

The lux value depends on two factors: the luminous intensity in candela and the distance from the light source to the illuminated surface.

lux = candela / distance²

Where:

  • lux = illuminance (lm/m²)
  • candela = luminous intensity (cd)
  • distance = in meters (m)

This formula assumes that the light hits the surface perpendicularly, and there is no beam spread or losses due to reflection or absorption.

Example Calculation

Let’s say you have a light with a luminous intensity of 1000 cd, and the distance to the wall is 2 meters.

lux = 1000 / (2 × 2) = 1000 / 4 = 250 lux

This means the wall receives 250 lux of illumination, which is suitable for general indoor lighting conditions.

Inverse Square Law of Light

The core of the conversion formula relies on the Inverse Square Law. As light travels further from its source, it spreads out and becomes less intense.

lux = cd / r² demonstrates that doubling the distance reduces illuminance by a factor of four.

Example:

  • At 1 meter: 1000 cd → 1000 lux
  • At 2 meters: 1000 cd → 250 lux
  • At 3 meters: 1000 cd → ~111 lux

This is essential knowledge for designing energy-efficient lighting systems.

Advanced Considerations

  • Angle of incidence: When the light hits the surface at an angle, effective lux decreases due to cosine law
  • Beam spread: Wider beams distribute intensity across a broader area, affecting local lux
  • Reflectivity: Reflective or matte surfaces may impact how light is perceived
  • Obstructions: Furniture, barriers, or people can block light, reducing effective lux

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Is lux the same as lumen?

No. Lumen is total light output, while lux is light per square meter (lumen/m²).

Q: Can I convert candela to lux without knowing the distance?

No. Distance is essential for this conversion as per the inverse square law.

Q: How many lux is 1 candela at 1 meter?

1 lux. That’s because 1 / 1² = 1.

Q: How do I measure lux in real life?

Use a light meter (lux meter), available as handheld devices or mobile apps.

Q: Why does light seem weaker over distance?

Because it spreads out in space and its intensity diminishes with the square of the distance.

Visualizing the Light: Beam Spread and Distribution

Understanding how light spreads from a source helps explain why lux decreases over distance. In reality, light travels in a cone or spherical pattern from a source. The farther it gets from the source, the wider the area it covers—and the thinner its intensity becomes. This concept is critical in lighting design.

For example, if a spotlight emits 1000 candela in a narrow beam, and that beam is projected 1 meter away, it illuminates a smaller area with high lux. At 5 meters, the same beam illuminates a larger area, but each part of that area receives less light per square meter.

To visualize this, imagine using a flashlight on a wall. At 1 meter, the beam is small and bright. As you step back to 3 or 4 meters, the beam gets wider and dimmer. This real-world effect is exactly what the inverse square law explains.

Practical Tips for Accurate Measurement

To ensure accurate lux estimation or measurement, consider the following practical guidelines:

  • Use a calibrated lux meter for high-accuracy readings
  • Account for surface reflectivity (white walls reflect more light than dark ones)
  • Adjust for beam angle and beam center fall-off (not all beams are perfectly even)
  • Ensure no obstructions between the light source and the measurement surface

Why Candela and Lux Are Both Needed

Many users wonder why both candela and lux are used in lighting terminology. They each serve a unique purpose:

  • Candela is ideal for understanding light **intensity** and direction
  • Lux helps evaluate how much light **actually reaches a surface**

This is especially important in fields like architecture, roadway safety, manufacturing, and retail display setups. For instance, a 500 cd spotlight and a 500 cd floodlight may seem equal, but due to different beam spreads, their lux values can be dramatically different at the same distance.
