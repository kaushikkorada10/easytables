What is Voltage Drop?

Voltage drop is the reduction in voltage as electrical current travels through a wire or cable. It is caused by the inherent resistance (or impedance) of the conductor. This loss becomes especially significant over long distances or when using cables with insufficient thickness for the given load.

The "2" accounts for the round trip (to and from the load).

Voltage Drop in Different Electrical Systems

Voltage drop behaves differently in various electrical systems. While the underlying principle remains the same, application and tolerance levels vary depending on whether the system is residential, commercial, or industrial.

  • Residential Systems: Typically operate at 120V or 240V (depending on region). Voltage drop is more noticeable in outdoor lighting, long extension cords, or remote loads like pumps.
  • Commercial Systems: Involves more complex three-phase loads, often at 208V, 400V, or 415V. Long cable runs in office buildings, elevators, and HVAC systems require precise voltage drop planning.
  • Industrial Systems: Often use higher voltage (e.g., 480V or 600V) and carry large currents over long distances. Voltage drop must be kept within tight tolerances to avoid machine errors or shutdowns.
