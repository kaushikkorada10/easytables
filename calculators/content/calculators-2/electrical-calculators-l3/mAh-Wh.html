mAh to Wh Calculator

Convert electric charge in milliamp-hours (mAh) to energy in watt-hours (Wh), using voltage.

Formula:

  • Wh = (mAh × V) / 1000

mAh to Wh Calculator – Convert Battery Capacity to Energy

The mAh to Wh Calculator is a powerful tool that helps convert milliamp-hours (mAh), a common unit for battery capacity, into watt-hours (Wh), which measures the actual energy a battery can store or deliver. This conversion is crucial for understanding how long a battery will last and for comparing different types of batteries, especially in consumer electronics, electric vehicles, and solar storage systems.

What is mAh (Milliamp-Hour)?

mAh stands for milliampere-hour. It measures electric charge and tells us how much current a battery can provide over a specific time. For example, a 2000 mAh battery can deliver 2000 milliamps for one hour or 1000 milliamps for two hours.

What is Wh (Watt-Hour)?

Wh represents the amount of energy used or stored. It is calculated by multiplying voltage (V) with amp-hours (Ah). Since mAh is a smaller unit, it must be converted to Ah first before using the formula:

Formula: mAh to Wh

Wh = (mAh × V) / 1000

This formula helps determine how much energy (in watt-hours) a battery can store, based on its capacity and voltage.

Example Calculation

If you have a 3000 mAh battery with a voltage of 3.7V, then:

Wh = (3000 × 3.7) / 1000 = 11.1 Wh

How to Use the mAh to Wh Calculator

  2. Enter the voltage (V) of the battery
  3. Click the "Convert" button to get the result in watt-hours (Wh)

Common Battery Use Cases

  • Smartphones: Typical battery 3000–5000 mAh @ 3.7V → ~11–18 Wh
  • Laptops: 4000–7000 mAh @ 11.1V → ~45–77 Wh
  • Drones: 2000–10000 mAh @ 11.1V → ~22–111 Wh
  • Power Banks: 10000–30000 mAh @ 3.7V → ~37–111 Wh
  • Electric Bikes: 10000–20000 mAh @ 36V → ~360–720 Wh

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

Q1: Can I convert mAh to Wh without knowing voltage?
No, voltage is essential in this calculation because energy depends on both charge and potential difference.

Q2: Is this calculator accurate for all types of batteries?
Yes, as long as the mAh and voltage inputs are accurate, this calculator works for lithium-ion, NiMH, lead-acid, and other battery types.

Q3: Why do airlines list battery restrictions in Wh and not mAh?
Because Wh gives a complete view of total energy stored, which is important for fire safety and transport regulations.

Best Practices for Battery Energy Planning

  • 🔋 Always check manufacturer-rated voltage before calculations
  • 🛑 Avoid pushing batteries to their maximum rated Wh capacity constantly
  • 📏 Use higher Wh ratings for longer backup and performance systems
  • 🧠 Understand that mAh alone does not reflect usable energy
