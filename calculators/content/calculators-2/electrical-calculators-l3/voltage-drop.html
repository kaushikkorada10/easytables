What Is Voltage Drop?

Voltage drop refers to the reduction in voltage that occurs as electrical current travels through a conductor (such as copper or aluminum wires). This happens because all conductors have some resistance, and when current flows through resistance, energy is lost as heat, resulting in a lower voltage at the load end.

Voltage Drop Formula

The voltage drop in a circuit can be calculated using the following formula:

Vdrop = (2 √ó I √ó L √ó R) / 1000

  • I = Current in amperes (A)
  • L = One-way length of the wire in meters (m)
  • R = Resistance per kilometer (Œ©/km)
  • Multiply by 2 to account for both the outgoing and return paths of current

Acceptable Voltage Drop Limits

Most standards recommend that the total voltage drop in a circuit should not exceed 3% to 5% of the nominal voltage. For a 230V system, a 3% voltage drop would be 6.9V. Anything more than this can affect the performance of sensitive devices.

Practical Voltage Drop Charts

Voltage drop tables are useful for quickly referencing allowable conductor lengths for given loads and wire sizes. These charts are often provided by wire manufacturers and show how far you can run a given cable size before voltage drop exceeds the recommended limit.

For example, for a 10 AWG copper wire carrying 20 amps at 120V, the maximum recommended length might be around 50 feet to stay within a 3% drop.

Regulatory and Code Compliance

In many countries, national electrical codes require compliance with voltage drop guidelines. For instance:

  • NEC (USA): Recommends 3% max drop for branch circuits and 5% total for feeders and branch
  • IEC (International): Similar guidance with specific country-level adaptations

Failing to follow these guidelines can lead to inspection failures or inefficient energy use.

Voltage Drop in DC Circuits

Voltage drop is even more critical in DC systems (solar panels, RVs, boats, etc.) where voltages are lower. A small loss in voltage can drastically reduce performance. Always oversize cables in low-voltage applications to keep losses under control.
