What Is kVA?

kVA (kilovolt-ampere) is a unit of apparent power in an electrical circuit. Apparent power is the product of voltage and current, without accounting for any phase difference (power factor) between them. It is used in AC (Alternating Current) systems and is important when designing transformers, generators, and backup systems.

1 kVA = 1,000 VA (volt-amperes)

What Is an Amp?

An ampere (amp) is the SI unit of electric current. It measures the amount of electrical charge flowing through a circuit per second. In AC systems, the current drawn by a device depends on the voltage and the power factor (PF), which reflects how efficiently the power is being used.

Importance of Power Factor

The power factor (PF) is a measure of how effectively electrical power is being converted into useful work. A power factor of 1 means all the power is being used effectively, while lower PFs indicate more energy losses. Commercial and industrial systems often operate at PFs between 0.7 and 0.95.

Using a realistic PF value improves calculation accuracy and helps in choosing the right wires, breakers, and transformers.
