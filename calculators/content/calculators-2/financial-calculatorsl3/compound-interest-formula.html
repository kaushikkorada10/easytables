What Is Compound Interest?

At its core, compound interest means reinvesting earned interest so that in subsequent periods, interest is calculated on both the original principal and the accumulated interest. Over time, this leads to exponential growth of your investment or loan balance.

Why Compound Interest Matters

Compound interest accelerates growth by reinvesting gains. Over decades, even a modest rate can dramatically increase your ending balance, making it a powerful tool for retirement savings, college funds, and wealth transfers.

Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Q: How often should I compound?

A: More frequent compounding yields marginally higher returns, but daily or monthly is sufficient for most savings and investment products.

Q: Can I compound interest on negative balances?

A: Yes‚Äîloans like mortgages compound interest on the outstanding balance, increasing the amount owed.

Q: What‚Äôs the difference between APR and APY?

A: APR (Annual Percentage Rate) excludes compounding, while APY (Annual Percentage Yield) includes it, reflecting true growth rate.

Q: How do I compare two compound rates?

A: Convert both to effective annual rate (EAR): EAR = (1 + r/n)n ‚Äì 1, then compare.

Q: Is continuous compounding always better?

A: The difference between daily and continuous compounding is minimal at typical rates‚Äîfocus on higher nominal rates instead.

Long-Term Impact of Early Investments

One of the most powerful benefits of compound interest is the impact of time. The earlier you start investing, the more time your money has to grow. For example, investing $5,000 annually from age 25 to 35 and then stopping will often yield more by retirement age than starting at 35 and contributing $5,000 annually until age 65. This is due to the compounding snowball effect that amplifies over time.

Role of Compound Interest in Retirement Planning

When planning for retirement, compound interest is your best ally. With long horizons and consistent contributions to retirement accounts (like IRAs, 401(k)s, PPF, or SIPs), your portfolio benefits greatly. Even small differences in interest rates or compounding frequencies can lead to tens of thousands of dollars in the long term.

Compound Interest with Inflation Adjustment
