What Is a Mortgage?

A mortgage is a long-term loan issued by a lender (typically a bank or financial institution) to help a borrower purchase a home or property. In return, the borrower agrees to repay the loan over time, usually in monthly installments, including interest charges. The property itself acts as collateral until the loan is fully repaid.

Key Components of a Mortgage

  • Loan Amount: The total amount you borrow after the down payment is subtracted.
  • Interest Rate: The annual cost of borrowing money, expressed as a percentage.
  • Loan Term: The number of years you agree to repay the mortgage (typically 15, 20, or 30 years).
  • Down Payment: The upfront payment made at the time of purchase, usually expressed as a percentage of the home price.
  • Property Taxes: Annual taxes paid to the government based on the property's value.
  • Homeowners Insurance: Required protection against damage, theft, and natural disasters.

Additional Costs to Consider

Monthly mortgage payments often include more than just principal and interest. You should also plan for:

  • Property Taxes: Vary by location and assessed home value
  • Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI): Required if down payment is less than 20%
  • Homeowners Insurance: Required by lenders to cover damage or liability
  • HOA Fees: Monthly dues required by homeowners associations
  • Maintenance and Repairs: Budget 1-3% of home value annually

  • Adjustable-rate Mortgage (ARM): Interest rate changes after a fixed period
  • APR: Annual Percentage Rate includes interest and other fees
  • Principal: The original loan amount borrowed
